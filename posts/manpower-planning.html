Chapter 02 · Workforce Planning

A Practical Guide to Manpower Planning

7 min read

Manpower planning often gets reduced to a headcount number in a budget spreadsheet: "we're approved for 40 more people this year." That number matters, but it's the output of a planning process, not the process itself. Done well, manpower planning answers a much more specific question — the right people, with the right skills, in the right roles, at the right time — and it connects hiring decisions directly to business strategy rather than to last year's headcount plus a growth percentage.

1. Start from demand, not from historical headcount

The most common shortcut in manpower planning is anchoring next year's headcount to this year's headcount and applying a growth or efficiency factor. It's fast, but it quietly carries forward every inefficiency and role that no longer matches the business's direction. A more useful starting point is workforce demand forecasting: working with business leaders to understand what the organisation needs to deliver over the next 12-24 months, and what that implies for roles, skills, and volume — not just for total numbers.

This usually means combining a few inputs:

  • Business plans and revenue/volume targets by function or business unit.
  • Productivity assumptions (e.g. how many units of output, customers served, or transactions processed per employee).
  • Strategic shifts — new markets, new products, automation of existing work — that change the roles needed, not just the count.

2. Map your supply before you plan your demand response

Supply analysis means understanding what you already have: current headcount by role and location, skill inventories, expected attrition, retirements, and internal mobility patterns. This is the step organisations most often skip or do superficially, which is a problem because the gap between demand and supply is the entire point of the exercise. A good supply analysis should be able to answer: which roles are we structurally short on even before we grow, where is attrition concentrated, and which skills are we quietly losing through retirements or turnover.

3. Do the gap analysis role by role, not just headline totals

A total headcount gap of zero can hide a significant problem — for example, a surplus of 20 in operational roles and a shortfall of 20 in technical roles that can't simply be redeployed against each other. Break the gap analysis down by role family, level, and location, and be explicit about which gaps can be closed through hiring, which through reskilling existing employees, and which through restructuring work (automation, outsourcing, or redesigning the role itself).

A useful lens: for every gap identified, ask whether the right response is "buy" (hire externally), "build" (develop existing employees), "borrow" (contractors, gig, or temporary talent), or "redesign" (change how the work gets done). Defaulting to "buy" for every gap is usually the most expensive option and the slowest to execute.

4. Build scenarios, not a single plan

Business conditions change, and a manpower plan built for a single scenario tends to fall apart the moment revenue misses target or a new project gets greenlit. Building 2-3 scenarios (e.g. base case, accelerated growth, slower growth) with pre-agreed triggers for which hiring or restructuring actions kick in under each gives the organisation a plan that can flex without a full re-planning cycle every time conditions shift.

5. Connect the plan to a real budget and a real hiring calendar

A manpower plan that lives only as a strategic document rarely survives contact with the annual budgeting process. To make it stick, translate the plan into a phased hiring calendar (which roles open when, not just how many roles this year) and tie it explicitly to the cost budget, so that finance and HR are working from the same numbers rather than reconciling two different plans midway through the year.

6. Revisit it quarterly, not annually

Manpower plans built once a year and left untouched go stale quickly, especially in fast-moving businesses. A lightweight quarterly review — checking actual hiring and attrition against plan, and adjusting for any material change in business direction — keeps the plan credible and prevents the year-end surprise of being significantly over or under where the business actually needed you to be.

Common pitfalls to watch for

  • Planning in absolute headcount only. Skills and role mix matter as much as the total number — a technically accurate headcount plan can still leave the business short of the capabilities it actually needs.
  • Ignoring internal supply. Internal mobility and reskilling are usually cheaper and faster than external hiring, but get overlooked when planning starts from a hiring mindset by default.
  • Treating the plan as fixed. The value of scenario planning is lost if the plan isn't actually revisited when the scenario changes.
