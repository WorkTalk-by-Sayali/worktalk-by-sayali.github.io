How to Do Rewards Benchmarking the Right Way

7 min read

"Let's benchmark our pay against the market" sounds like a simple ask. In practice, it involves a series of judgment calls — which companies count as your market, how you match your jobs to survey jobs, where you want to sit relative to that market, and how you turn a benchmarking report into an actual pay decision. Get these wrong and you end up with a benchmarking exercise that produces a number nobody quite trusts.

1. Get clear on your comparator set before you look at any data

The single biggest driver of your benchmarking result is which companies you compare yourself against — not the survey provider, not the methodology. A comparator set that's too broad (all companies in the country) will dilute meaningful comparisons; one that's too narrow (only direct competitors) may not have enough data points to be statistically reliable. A workable comparator set is usually defined along a few dimensions together: industry or sector, company size (revenue or headcount), and geography — and it's worth revisiting this set periodically as the business's real talent competitors shift, which they often do faster than people expect.

2. Job matching matters more than the survey you choose

Compensation surveys are only as good as the job matching behind them. Matching should be done based on the actual scope, level, and accountabilities of the role — not the job title. Two "Manager" titles in the same company can have very different scope, and matching them to the same survey benchmark will quietly distort your results. Where possible, use a consistent job leveling framework internally first, so that benchmarking is comparing like-for-like levels of accountability rather than titles that mean different things across teams.

A common mistake: matching a role to the survey job with the closest-sounding title rather than the closest-matching scope. When in doubt, match on what the role is actually accountable for, not what it's called.

3. Decide your positioning philosophy before you see the numbers

Where you want to sit against the market — median (P50), above median (P60-P75), or a blend depending on role criticality — is a policy decision, not something that should be decided reactively once benchmarking data comes back. Organisations that skip this step tend to make positioning decisions role-by-role under pressure, which quietly erodes internal consistency and is hard to defend when employees compare notes. It's also common, and reasonable, to position differently by role criticality — for example, benchmarking hard-to-hire technical or leadership roles closer to P75 while positioning more readily available roles closer to P50.

4. Look at total rewards, not just base pay

A benchmarking exercise that only compares base salary can miss a large part of the real market position, especially where variable pay, equity, or benefits make up a meaningful share of total compensation in your industry. Where possible, benchmark total cash (base plus target variable pay) at minimum, and total rewards (including benefits and any equity) where the data allows — a role that looks underpaid on base alone may be well-positioned once variable pay is included, or vice versa.

5. Balance external competitiveness with internal equity

External market data tells you what the market pays; it doesn't tell you what's fair inside your own organisation. Adjusting pay purely to match external benchmarks, role by role, without checking internal equity can create situations where two employees doing comparably scoped work end up with very different pay simply because one role's external market moved more than the other's. Internal equity reviews — comparing pay across similar levels and roles within the organisation — should run alongside external benchmarking, not as an afterthought once external adjustments are already made.

6. Turn the report into decisions, not just a reference document

A benchmarking report that sits in a folder doesn't change anything. The useful output is a clear set of actions: which roles or levels are meaningfully out of line with your positioning philosophy, what the cost of closing those gaps would be, and a phased approach if closing them all in one cycle isn't affordable. Benchmarking works best as an input to the annual compensation review cycle, not as a standalone project disconnected from when pay decisions actually get made.

7. Refresh on a sensible cycle

Annual refreshes are standard for most roles, but fast-moving talent markets (certain technical or specialist roles, for instance) may need more frequent pulse checks rather than waiting for the next annual cycle to discover a role has drifted significantly below market.

Common pitfalls to watch for

  • Changing comparator sets year to year without a clear reason. This makes trend data unreliable and invites (fair) questions about whether the comparator set was chosen to justify a predetermined answer.
  • Benchmarking base pay only in industries where variable pay is a large share of compensation. This systematically misreads your actual market position.
  • Treating positioning philosophy as fixed forever. It's worth revisiting positioning periodically as the business's talent strategy and affordability change, rather than assuming a policy set five years ago still fits.
